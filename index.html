Generalization and Adaptation in the Era of Foundation Models

From pretraining to deployment: understanding how foundation models generalize, adapt, and fail — and determining when they can be trusted.

🗓 Date December 2026
📍 Location NeurIPS 2026 (TBD)
📝 Format Full-day, in-person
Call for Papers dates + guidelines Program schedule Organizers committee

Overview

Foundation Models (FMs), pretrained on vast and diverse datasets, increasingly span language, vision, multimodal, scientific, and tabular domains. As they move beyond curated benchmarks into open-world, safety-critical, and agentic settings, two intertwined challenges dominate the research frontier: how FMs generalize beyond their training distributions, and how they can be adapted efficiently, continually, and reliably to novel tasks, modalities, users, and environments.

This workshop views generalization and adaptation not as isolated tracks, but as interconnected stages within a unified deployment lifecycle: pretraining → generalization → adaptation → continual learning. It brings together researchers from computer vision, NLP, multimodal learning, ML theory, AI for science, and AI safety to advance the theoretical understanding and methodological foundations needed for FMs that remain reliable under distribution shifts and continually changing real-world environments.

Three Pillars

Foundations of Generalization

Why and when FMs generalize across domains, modalities, tasks, and environments — emergent and in-context generalization, theory and mechanistic understanding, the role of pretraining data, and robustness under distribution shifts.

Adaptation Mechanisms

How FMs adapt and improve after deployment — parameter-efficient fine-tuning, model merging, test-time adaptation, inference-time scaling, retrieval-augmented and continual learning, and agentic adaptation via tools and memory.

Applications & Evaluation

Deployment and assessment in high-impact domains — robotics, scientific discovery, time-series, biomedicine, and remote sensing — with contamination-resistant benchmarks and rigorous empirical validation.
